# Conceptual Core

Recursive Semantic Infrastructure (RSI) is built on the premise that language can be modeled as a recursive field of interacting semantic regions rather than as a flat sequence of tokens or a purely symbolic logic stream.

This document defines the first-principles conceptual core of the project.

---

## 1. Primary Question

RSI begins with the following question:

> What if meaning is not primarily a symbolic label or a logical proposition, but a stable geometric structure that emerges under recursive constraint interaction?

This project answers that question by treating language as a structured field in which semantic possibilities are shaped, constrained, stabilized, and transformed.

---

## 2. Core Ontological Shift

The foundational shift in RSI is from:

- token → region
- relation → interference
- proposition → basin
- certainty → viable coherence
- meaning → persistent structural agreement

This shift does not reject symbolic language. It reframes symbolic language as a surface encoding of deeper semantic structure.

---

## 3. Terms as Regions

A term is treated as a region in semantic possibility space.

A term is not assumed to be:
- a fixed point
- a fully discrete symbol
- a complete meaning in itself

Instead, a term is understood as:
- a structured possibility region
- a latent semantic envelope
- a potentially ambiguous domain of viable interpretation

This means that terms do not carry fully fixed meaning independently. Their meaning emerges through interaction with other terms and constraints.

### Consequences
- ambiguity is natural
- terms may overlap
- terms may differentiate under constraint
- terms may collapse into one another when not distinguishable
- terms may split when asymmetry forces new distinctions

---

## 4. Language as Recursive Constraint Interaction

Language is modeled as recursive interference among semantic regions.

Terms do not simply "connect." They constrain one another’s possible interpretation.

At the deepest level, semantic structure emerges through repeated application of constraint over possibility regions.

This means that language is not modeled primarily as:
- syntax trees
- fixed propositions
- sentence-level truth containers

It is modeled as:
- recursive semantic transformation
- viability shaping
- interference among regions
- persistent agreement or failure under recurrence

---

## 5. Constraint as the Fundamental Operation

Constraint is the basic semantic operation.

A constraint acts on one or more regions by altering their viability within a context.

Constraint is the deeper layer beneath familiar semantic operations such as:
- attribution
- conditioning
- comparison
- negation
- reference
- causal relation

These are not treated as ultimate primitives. They are treated as structured expressions of more basic constraint behavior.

### Constraint can act by:
- reinforcing viable structure
- reducing viable structure
- reshaping viable structure
- ordering viable structure
- anchoring viable structure

In this framework, meaning is what remains viable after recursive constraint interaction.

---

## 6. Asymmetry as the Origin of Structure

Distinguishability begins with asymmetry.

If all regions respond identically to all constraints, then no semantic structure can emerge.

The first condition for structure is:

> asymmetry in constraint response

That means:
- some regions respond differently than others
- viability becomes non-uniform
- distinction becomes possible

From this, the system gains:
- distinguishability
- basis formation
- dimensional emergence
- geometry
- coherent structure

### Foundational statement
> Structure begins when asymmetry produces differential viability response.

---

## 7. Distinguishability and Basis Formation

A region becomes distinguishable when at least one constraint produces a different viability response in it than in another region.

Two regions are indistinguishable when no available constraint can differentiate them.

Basis regions are therefore not defined primarily by size or stability, but by distinguishability.

### Basis region
A basis region is the smallest region that constraints can meaningfully differentiate under the current system.

This means:
- basis is relative to available constraints
- basis may refine under new asymmetries
- basis may collapse when distinctions become redundant
- basis is dynamic, not absolutely fixed

### Foundational statement
> Basis regions are the smallest units of semantic space that constraints can meaningfully differentiate.

---

## 8. Viability as the Central Observable

Viability is the central observable of RSI.

Rather than asking:
- is this true?
- is this false?

RSI first asks:
- what semantic structure survives recursive constraint interaction?
- where does coherent density accumulate?
- what remains stable enough to count as a basin?

### Viability
Viability is the coherent density of a semantic region that survives recursive constraint interference.

Viability is not identical to:
- truth
- frequency
- confidence
- empirical probability

At this stage, viability is structural.

It describes:
- what remains possible
- what remains coherent
- what remains supported by compatible constraints

### Structural probability
Structural probability can be understood as the remaining viable freedom within constrained possibility space.

---

## 9. Coherence as Persistent Dynamic Constraint Agreement

Coherence is not merely local alignment.

A basin is coherent only if aligned constraint responses persist across recursive application.

### Coherence
Coherence is the persistence of aligned constraint responses under recursive application.

This means coherence requires:
- compatibility
- recurrence stability
- basin continuity
- lawful persistence through transformation

A structure may be viable but not coherent if it forms briefly and collapses under recurrence.

### Foundational statement
> Meaning requires not only viability, but persistent dynamic constraint agreement.

---

## 10. Claims as Emergent Basins

Claims are not treated as primitive logical atoms.

Claims emerge as coherent basins of viability under recursive semantic interaction.

A claim is therefore not first defined as:
- a sentence
- a proposition
- a grammatical unit

It is defined as:
- a stable or semi-stable semantic basin
- a concentrated region of coherent viability
- a structure that persists under recursive constraint agreement

### Consequences
- claims may grow
- claims may thicken
- claims may branch
- claims may distort
- claims may rupture
- claims may collapse

This allows claims to behave like dynamical semantic structures rather than fixed symbolic containers.

---

## 11. Dimensions as Emergent Directions of Constraint

Dimensions are not assumed as fixed containers.

A semantic dimension emerges when consistent ordering or differential viability response appears across regions.

Dimensions are therefore understood as:
- stable directions of constraint response
- emergent axes of ordering
- structured differences revealed by repeated comparison or alignment

Dimensions are not identical to terms.

Terms occupy regions.
Dimensions describe ways regions vary or are ordered.

### Foundational statement
> A dimension is a stable direction in the space of viability transformations.

---

## 12. Geometry from Constraint Ordering

Once dimensions emerge, semantic space becomes geometric.

At that point, the system can support:
- direction
- angle
- alignment
- opposition
- orthogonality
- ordering
- relative metric structure

This allows:
- comparison to create ordering geometry
- modality to scale amplitude
- negation to subtract or oppose regions
- dimensions to define semantic directions
- angles to represent alignment of constraint vectors

### Foundational statement
> Semantic geometry emerges when consistent constraint orderings produce stable directions of variation.

---

## 13. Recursive Semantic Waveform Framing

RSI may be represented dynamically as semantic waveform recursion.

This means:
- regions are treated as structured possibility envelopes
- constraints act as interference modes
- modality acts like amplitude weighting
- polarity acts like phase alignment or opposition
- recurrence tests persistence
- viability forms coherence volume

This waveform framing is not the ontological base by itself. It is a dynamic representation of the deeper constraint topology.

### Important distinction
- topology is primary
- waveform dynamics are representational and dynamical

This keeps the system from collapsing into metaphor while preserving the power of signal-language mapping.

---

## 14. Failure Regimes and Field Regimes

RSI distinguishes between low-structure field states and failures of formed structures.

### Field regimes
These describe the condition of the semantic field:
- noise
- uncertainty
- chaos

### Failure regimes
These describe failure of coherent structure under recurrence:
- collapse
- distortion
- rupture

This distinction matters because:
- uncertainty is not necessarily failure
- low-density structure may still be viable
- failure implies loss of persistence or resolvability

### Foundational statement
> Noise, uncertainty, and chaos describe the state of the field; collapse, distortion, and rupture describe the failure of formed structure.

---

## 15. Expansion and Compression

RSI assumes that semantic structure adapts through expansion and compression of its basis.

### Compression
Compression occurs when regions are redundantly represented and can be safely merged without loss of distinguishability.

### Expansion
Expansion occurs when unresolved asymmetry exceeds current basis resolution and new distinctions are required.

This means the system tends toward:
- maximal distinguishability
- under coherent stability
- with minimal unnecessary complexity

### Foundational statement
> Semantic infrastructure expands to resolve incoherent asymmetry and compresses redundant symmetry to preserve coherent distinguishability.

---

## 16. Relationship to Structural Memory

RSI is compatible with broader structural memory work because both systems are fundamentally concerned with:
- persistence
- recurrence
- basin formation
- lawful transformation
- constraint-driven structure
- coherence under recursive application

RSI differs by focusing specifically on semantic and language-space structure.

It can therefore serve as:
- a semantic substrate
- a language-space geometric framework
- a bridge from dynamical signal structure into semantic viability structure

---

## 17. Provisional Core Summary

The conceptual core of RSI can be summarized as follows:

1. Terms are regions in semantic possibility space.
2. Language is recursive interference among semantic regions.
3. Constraint is the fundamental semantic operation.
4. Asymmetry generates distinguishability.
5. Distinguishability produces basis regions.
6. Viability is the coherent density that survives recursive interference.
7. Coherence is persistent dynamic constraint agreement.
8. Claims are emergent semantic basins.
9. Dimensions are emergent directions of viability transformation.
10. Meaning is the persistence of viable coherent structure under recursive constraint interaction.

---

## 18. Provisional One-Line Core

> Recursive Semantic Infrastructure models meaning as persistent coherent viability emerging from recursive constraint interaction over semantic regions.
