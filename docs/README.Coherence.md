Coherence

This document formalizes coherence as the primary stability condition of semantic structure in Recursive Semantic Infrastructure (RSI).

Where viability describes what survives, coherence describes what remains stably organized as itself under recursive constraint application.

Coherence is the condition that allows:

basins to persist
dimensions to stabilize
comparison to remain meaningful
semantic geometry to form
meaning to exist as more than transient structure
1. Purpose

RSI requires a concept that answers:

when does viable structure become stable enough to count as meaning?
when does a basin remain itself across recurrence?
when do constraints agree rather than conflict?
when does structure resist fragmentation, distortion, or collapse?

Coherence is that concept.

2. Core Definition
2.1 Provisional definition

Coherence is the persistence of aligned constraint responses under recursive application.

This means coherence requires:

alignment of constraint effects
persistence across recurrence layers
sufficient support density to stabilize structure
2.2 Foundational statement

Coherence is not mere compatibility; it is persistent dynamic agreement of constraint effects over time.

3. Coherence vs Viability

RSI distinguishes clearly between viability and coherence.

3.1 Viability
what survives constraint interaction
may be diffuse, fragmented, or unstable
can exist without persistence
3.2 Coherence
what remains stably aligned under recurrence
requires persistence
requires constraint agreement
requires sufficient density
3.3 Relationship
viability is the substrate
coherence is the stability condition
3.4 Foundational statement

Viability allows structure to exist; coherence allows structure to remain itself.

6. Coherence and Dimensional Stability

You pushed something very real earlier:

dimensions = stable ordering regions

Now we can formalize:

6.1 Dimension stability condition

A dimension exists only if coherence is maintained along its ordering axis.

That means:

ordering must persist across recurrence
constraint responses must remain consistent
comparison must remain stable
6.2 Dimensional collapse

A dimension collapses when:

ordering becomes inconsistent
constraint responses diverge
coherence along that axis falls below threshold
6.3 Foundational statement

A dimension is a coherence-stabilized direction of constraint response.

7. Coherence and Independence (Orthogonality)

Your intuition here was sharp:

independence = consistent ordering that doesn’t interfere

We can now define:

7.1 Orthogonality condition

Two dimensions are orthogonal when:

constraint responses do not systematically co-vary
coherence in one dimension does not disrupt another
7.2 Coherence-based independence

Dimensions are independent when:

their coherence fields do not interfere destructively
alignment remains local to each axis
7.3 Failure case

Loss of independence occurs when:

constraints begin coupling across axes
coherence in one dimension distorts another

This produces:

dimensional collapse
distortion
reduced volume
