# Glossary

This glossary defines the working vocabulary of Recursive Semantic Infrastructure (RSI).

The goal of this document is not to finalize every term permanently, but to stabilize the current ontology enough that future formalization, experimentation, and implementation do not drift from the conceptual core.

Where possible, definitions are written to be:
- structurally precise
- recursively compatible
- minimal without being vague
- consistent with the current RSI conceptual core

---

## A

### Alignment
The degree to which active constraints produce compatible directional effects on viability.

Alignment is a component of coherence and contributes to whether a basin converges or fragments.

High alignment means:
- constraints reinforce similar viable regions
- ordering relations are compatible
- destructive interference is limited or structured

Low alignment means:
- constraints diverge
- multiple incompatible viability peaks compete
- persistent basin formation is weakened

---

### Amplitude
The magnitude of a constraint’s effect on viability.

In RSI’s waveform framing, amplitude corresponds to constraint strength. Modality acts as amplitude scaling.

Examples:
- “is slow” implies stronger amplitude than “may be slow”
- “likely” implies higher amplitude than “possibly”

---

### Asymmetry
Differential constraint response across regions.

Asymmetry is the minimal condition for distinguishability. If all regions respond identically to all constraints, no structure can emerge.

Foundationally:
- asymmetry produces differential viability response
- differential viability response produces distinction
- distinction produces basis and geometry

---

## B

### Basis
The current set of smallest distinguishable semantic units available under a constraint system.

Basis is not absolute. It is relative to:
- available constraints
- current resolution
- active distinguishability
- current scale of analysis

Basis may expand when unresolved asymmetry requires new distinctions.
Basis may compress when previously distinct regions become redundant.

---

### Basis expansion
The refinement or increase of basis complexity in response to unresolved asymmetry that exceeds current resolution.

Expansion is typically triggered when:
- coherence drops
- distortions appear
- distinctions are needed to preserve resolvability

---

### Basis compression
The lawful merging or simplification of basis regions when distinctions are redundant and coherence can be preserved without loss of meaningful resolution.

Compression occurs when:
- regions respond similarly across constraints
- symmetry is high
- representation can be simplified without destroying distinguishability

---

### Basis region
The smallest semantic region that constraints can meaningfully differentiate under the current system.

A basis region is defined by distinguishability, not by size or stability alone.

---

### Basin
A coherent semantic structure formed by concentrated viability under a generating constraint field.

A basin is not a primitive symbolic unit. It is an emergent structure.

A basin may:
- form
- persist
- thicken
- branch
- drift
- distort
- rupture
- collapse

---

### Basin identity
The persistent dimensional constraint-response signature that allows a basin to remain recognizably itself across recursive transformation.

Basin identity is not defined by surface wording alone. It depends on:
- viability regime
- constraint response pattern
- dimensional loading
- lineage continuity

---

### Basin tuple
The minimal state representation of a basin at recurrence layer \(t\).

Current working form:

\[
B_t = (V_t,\; C_t,\; R_t,\; L_t)
\]

Where:
- \(V_t\) = viability distribution
- \(C_t\) = active constraint field
- \(R_t\) = dimensional response signature
- \(L_t\) = lineage trace

---

## C

### Causal coupling
A special class of relational constraint in which viability change in one region conditions or predicts viability change in another.

Causal coupling is not treated as an ultimate primitive. It is a structured form of coupling constraint that preserves continuity of viability transformation.

---

### Chaos
A low-persistence, highly sensitive structural regime in which local patterns form and break rapidly under recursive application.

Chaos is a field regime, not necessarily a total failure state.

Chaos differs from rupture in that:
- chaos is unstable but continuous
- rupture is discontinuous identity break

---

### Claim
An emergent coherent basin of semantic viability.

A claim is not first defined as:
- a sentence
- a proposition
- a grammar object

A claim is defined as:
- a stable or semi-stable viability basin
- a concentrated semantic structure
- a region that persists under recursive constraint agreement

---

### Coherence
The persistence of aligned constraint responses under recursive application.

Coherence is stronger than local compatibility. It requires:
- agreement
- recurrence stability
- continuity
- lawful persistence across layers

Current working minimal model:

\[
C = A \cdot P \cdot D
\]

Where:
- \(A\) = alignment / convergence
- \(P\) = persistence
- \(D\) = compatible support density

---

### Collapse
Loss of basin viability below the threshold of meaningful distinguishability.

Collapse occurs when a coherent structure can no longer preserve enough viable density or continuity to remain structurally present.

Collapse is a failure regime.

---

### Comparison
A relational constraint that imposes directional ordering between regions along an emergent dimension.

Comparison does not merely select a region. It creates or reveals geometry by ordering regions relative to one another.

Examples:
- faster than
- more likely than
- worse than

---

### Compatible support density
The concentration of mutually compatible supporting constraints within a basin.

This is the \(D\) term in the current minimal coherence model.

High density means:
- constraints overlap constructively
- support is thick rather than sparse
- local viability is strongly concentrated

---

### Compression
See: **Basis compression**

---

### Constraint
The fundamental semantic operation in RSI.

A constraint alters viability by reshaping semantic possibility regions.

Constraint is the deeper layer beneath familiar language operations such as:
- attribution
- conditioning
- comparison
- negation
- reference
- causal relation

Constraint may:
- reinforce
- reduce
- reshape
- order
- anchor

---

### Constraint agreement
The degree to which active constraints remain mutually compatible in their effect on viability.

Constraint agreement is a core part of coherence and persistence.

---

### Constraint field
The set of active constraints and their weighted effects over semantic regions at a given recurrence layer.

The constraint field is represented in the basin tuple as \(C_t\).

---

### Constraint lineage
The trace of which prior constraints, parents, or recurrence paths lawfully generated a current basin.

Constraint lineage is important because two basins may look similar while arising from different generative histories.

---

### Constraint polarity
The directional sign of a constraint’s effect on viability.

In waveform language, polarity corresponds to phase alignment or opposition.

Broadly:
- constructive polarity increases or reinforces viability
- destructive polarity reduces or opposes viability

---

### Constraint strength
The magnitude of a constraint’s effect on viability.

Constraint strength is closely related to amplitude in the waveform framing.

---

### Constraint symmetry
The degree to which multiple regions respond similarly to the same constraint conditions.

High symmetry may support compression.
Low symmetry may support expansion.

---

### Constructive interference
Constraint interaction that reinforces or increases coherent viability in a region.

Constructive interference produces:
- stronger basins
- higher support density
- more stable local coherence

---

## D

### Density
The concentration of compatible viability support in a local region or basin.

Density is not the same as truth or certainty. It describes how thickly supported a viable semantic structure is.

---

### Dimension
A stable direction in the space of viability transformations.

A dimension emerges when consistent ordering or differential response appears across regions.

Dimensions are not terms.
Terms occupy regions.
Dimensions describe ways regions vary, align, or are ordered.

---

### Dimensional response signature
The characteristic pattern by which a basin responds across its defining dimensions.

This is the \(R_t\) component of the basin tuple and is central to basin identity.

---

### Distinguishability
The ability of available constraints to produce different viability responses across regions.

If two regions respond identically under all available constraints, they are indistinguishable.

---

### Distortion
Loss of faithful structural resolvability because asymmetry exceeds the current basis resolution.

Distortion does not necessarily mean structure vanishes.
It means the system can no longer resolve structure lawfully at its current basis.

Distortion is a failure regime.

---

### Drift
The differential transformation of a basin’s viability geometry, generating constraints, and dimensional response across recurrence layers.

Drift includes:
- viability center shift
- basin shape shift
- dominant constraint shift

Drift may be lawful development or may signal destabilization, depending on overlap and identity retention.

---

### Dynamic range
The band of asymmetry within which distinguishability can be resolved coherently by the current basis.

Below this band:
- structure collapses toward symmetry / uniformity

Above this band:
- distortion, fragmentation, or collapse may occur

Meaning lives within the resolvable dynamic range of asymmetry.

---

## E

### Empirical probability
Probability derived from recurrence, observation, signal frequency, or measured external behavior.

Empirical probability is distinct from RSI’s structural probability and belongs more directly to dynamical/observational systems such as DME.

---

### Equivalence class
A set of regions that remain indistinguishable under all currently available constraints.

Equivalence classes define the effective partitioning of semantic space under the current basis.

---

### Expansion
See: **Basis expansion**

---

## F

### Failure regime
A regime in which a previously formed or forming structure cannot preserve coherence, identity, or lawful resolvability.

Current failure regimes include:
- collapse
- distortion
- rupture

---

### Field regime
A general condition of the semantic field rather than a failure of a specific formed basin.

Current field regimes include:
- noise
- uncertainty
- chaos

---

### Fragmentation
The breaking of viability concentration into multiple weakly connected or competing sub-basins.

Fragmentation often accompanies:
- low alignment
- low persistence
- high unresolved asymmetry

---

## G

### Geometry
The ordered, directional, and metric structure that emerges once dimensions are formed through repeated constraint orderings and differential response.

Geometry in RSI includes:
- direction
- alignment
- opposition
- orthogonality
- angle
- relative ordering

---

## I

### Identity
The invariant structural signature that allows a basin to remain itself through recursive transformation.

In RSI, identity is not mere lexical repetition. It is the persistence of:
- response regime
- dimensional loading
- generative continuity
- basin character

---

### Indistinguishability
The condition in which two regions cannot be differentially transformed by any currently available constraint.

Two regions are indistinguishable when:
- no constraint produces a differential viability response between them
- they collapse into the same effective basis class

---

### Interference
The interaction of constraints over regions in a way that reshapes viability.

Interference may be:
- constructive
- destructive
- mixed / transformative

Interference is the general dynamic language for how constraint effects combine.

---

## L

### Layer
A recurrence level or structural level of evaluation within RSI.

Common uses include:
- recurrence layer
- scale layer
- structural layer
- dynamical layer

Layer should always be qualified by context when possible.

---

### Lineage
The provenance trail through which a basin, region, or structure inherits its lawful continuity from earlier states.

Lineage is represented in the basin tuple as \(L_t\).

---

### Low-density structure
A regime in which viability is present but weakly concentrated.

Low-density structure may still be meaningful and should not automatically be treated as failure.

Uncertainty is one important case of low-density structure.

---

## M

### Meaning
The persistence of viable coherent structure under recursive constraint interaction.

Meaning is not treated as a primitive symbolic given. It is an emergent result of recursive viability and coherence.

---

### Modality
Directional amplitude modulation of a constraint toward or away from a region.

Modality does not usually change what region is targeted. It changes how strongly that targeting is enforced.

Examples:
- may
- might
- likely
- probably
- possibly

---

## N

### Negation
A destructive or subtractive constraint acting on a region, relation, or ordering.

Negation usually removes or opposes viability but does not by itself positively specify a replacement basin.

Examples:
- not slow
- not more likely
- not caused by

---

### Noise
Diffuse low-density perturbation without stable basin concentration.

Noise is a field regime.

Noise differs from uncertainty in that uncertainty retains directional bias, while noise may be more weakly structured or more diffuse.

---

## O

### Orthogonality
The condition in which dimensions or constraint directions do not systematically co-vary in their effects on viability.

Orthogonality is the basis of dimensional independence.

---

### Overlap
The retained multi-scale continuity of viable region, generating constraints, response signature, and lineage between basin states.

Overlap is not merely lexical similarity. It includes:
- viability overlap
- constraint overlap
- response overlap
- lineage continuity

---

### Ordering
A relational structuring of regions along an emergent dimension.

Ordering is produced by comparison and differential constraint response.

---

## P

### Persistence
The degree to which a viable basin preserves its identity, agreement, and geometry across recurrence layers.

Current working recurrence step:

\[
P_t = O_t \cdot G_t \cdot (1 - D_t)
\]

Where:
- \(O_t\) = overlap across layers
- \(G_t\) = retention of core constraint agreement
- \(D_t\) = normalized drift

Persistence differs from mere viability because it requires recurrence stability.

---

### Phase
The directional alignment or opposition of a constraint relative to others in the waveform framing.

Phase is closely tied to polarity and is useful when representing semantic dynamics as interference patterns.

---

### Polarity
See: **Constraint polarity**

---

### Probability manifold
A way of describing semantic possibility space when viability is treated as structured probability under recursive constraint interaction.

In RSI, structural probability refers to viable freedom within constrained space, not empirical frequency.

---

### Projection
A constraint act that selects or biases a term or region toward a sub-region or state.

Projection underlies many familiar semantic behaviors such as attribution or directional selection.

---

## R

### Recurrence
The repeated application of constraint structure across layers.

Recurrence is what allows RSI to test:
- persistence
- coherence
- identity retention
- drift
- rupture

---

### Recurrence layer
A single pass of recursive semantic reapplication.

Typical interpretations:
- direct local interaction
- reapplication to an assembled basin
- paraphrase or closure reapplication
- neighboring implication or structural echo

---

### Recursive semantic waveform recursion
A dynamic framing in which semantic regions are treated as structured possibility envelopes and constraints are treated as interference modes.

This is a representation of semantic dynamics, not the sole ontological base of RSI.

---

### Reference
A constraint or anchoring act that links a current region or expression to a prior semantic identity.

Reference stabilizes continuity and reduces uncontrolled drift.

Examples:
- this issue
- that pattern
- the same slowdown

---

### Region
A possibility envelope in semantic space.

A region is not necessarily sharply bounded. It may be:
- broad
- narrow
- ambiguous
- overlapping
- conditionally active

Terms are modeled as regions.

---

### Relation
A higher-order or familiar language-level description of structure that is often reducible to deeper constraint behavior.

RSI treats many familiar relations as derived expressions rather than ultimate primitives.

---

### Resolvability
The degree to which current basis structure can meaningfully differentiate and preserve distinctions under active asymmetry.

Distortion occurs when asymmetry exceeds resolvability.

---

### Rupture
A discontinuous break in basin identity across recursive layers.

Rupture is different from chaos or drift because it marks a sharp discontinuity rather than unstable but continuous transformation.

---

## S

### Scale
The level of resolution or granularity at which a region, basin, or distinction is being evaluated.

Scale matters because:
- overlap may differ by scale
- basis may refine or collapse by scale
- distortion may appear at one scale but not another

---

### Semantic field
The active space of terms, regions, constraints, and viability within which semantic structures form.

---

### Semantic geometry
The directional and ordered structure that emerges once semantic dimensions and viability transformations become stable enough to support geometric interpretation.

---

### Structural probability
The remaining viable freedom within constrained semantic possibility space.

Structural probability is not empirical frequency. It is viability-relative possibility under current constraints.

---

### Support density
See: **Compatible support density**

---

### Symmetry
Uniformity of constraint response across regions.

Excess symmetry produces indistinguishability and reduces structure.
Excess asymmetry may exceed basis resolution and produce distortion.

---

## T

### Term
A region in semantic possibility space.

A term is not treated as a fixed symbolic point but as a structured possibility envelope whose meaning emerges through recursive constraint interaction.

---

### Topology
The structural arrangement of regions, overlaps, containments, gates, and continuity relations prior to or beneath full metric geometry.

In RSI:
- topology is primary
- waveform dynamics are representational
- geometry emerges from stable orderings and differential response

---

### Transformative interference
A mixed pattern of constructive and destructive interference that reshapes a region while preserving some continuity of identity.

Transformation is not treated as a separate primitive from constructive and destructive interference; it is their structured composition.

---

## U

### Uncertainty
Low-density but directionally biased structure.

Uncertainty is not absence of structure. It is a weakly concentrated viable regime that remains partially open.

Uncertainty is a field regime, not automatically a failure regime.

---

## V

### Viability
The coherent density of a semantic region that survives recursive constraint interference.

Viability is the central observable of RSI.

It answers:
- what remains possible?
- what remains coherent?
- what survives recursive constraint application?

Viability is structural, not yet empirical.

---

### Viability distribution
The structured density pattern of viability over regions or basis regions at a given recurrence layer.

This is the \(V_t\) component of the basin tuple.

A viability distribution may be described by:
- center
- spread
- density
- fragmentation

---

### Viability transformation
Any constraint-induced change in the structure, concentration, or ordering of viable semantic regions.

Dimensions are emergent stable directions in the space of viability transformations.

---

## W

### Waveform framing
A representational framing in which semantic dynamics are described using concepts such as:
- amplitude
- phase
- interference
- modulation
- coherence volume

This framing is useful when dynamically modeling semantic behavior, but it is not the sole ontological base of RSI.

---

## X

### Axis
See: **Dimension**

## Z

### Zero-density region
A region that has lost viable support under the current constraint field.

A zero-density region may:
- be excluded by negation
- collapse under contradiction
- remain unreachable under current basis resolution
