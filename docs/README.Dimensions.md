# Dimensions

This document formalizes dimensions in Recursive Semantic Infrastructure (RSI).

Dimensions are not treated as pre-given containers or fixed abstract coordinates.
They are treated as emergent, coherence-stabilized directions in the space of viability transformations.

This document defines:

- what a dimension is
- how dimensions emerge
- how dimensions differ from terms and regions
- how ordering creates dimensional structure
- how independence and orthogonality arise
- how dimensions stabilize, collapse, or distort
- how dimensions relate to basin identity and semantic geometry

---

## 1. Purpose

RSI requires a lawful way to represent semantic directionality.

Without dimensions, RSI can model:
- regions
- constraints
- viability
- basins

But it cannot yet lawfully formalize:
- comparison
- direction
- ordering
- angle
- orthogonality
- metric structure
- basin response identity across axes

Dimensions provide that missing structure.

---

## 2. Core Definition

### 2.1 Provisional definition

A dimension is a stable direction in the space of viability transformations.

This means a dimension is not:
- a fixed symbolic category
- a pre-imposed container
- a term in the same sense as an ordinary semantic region

A dimension is:
- an emergent axis of ordered variation
- a stable direction of differential constraint response
- a coherence-supported ordering structure

---

### 2.2 Foundational statement

> A dimension is a coherence-stabilized direction of viability transformation.

---

## 3. Dimensions Are Not Terms

RSI distinguishes clearly between terms and dimensions.

### 3.1 Terms
Terms occupy semantic regions.

A term is:
- a possibility envelope
- a semantic region
- a potentially ambiguous unit of meaning-space

### 3.2 Dimensions
Dimensions describe ways regions vary, align, or are ordered.

A dimension is:
- a direction of variation
- a stable ordering axis
- an emergent structure over regions

---

### 3.3 Consequence

Terms and dimensions are related but not identical.

- terms provide the material of semantic space
- dimensions provide the directional structure of semantic transformation

---

### 3.4 Important clarification

A dimension may be named using language such as:
- speed
- reliability
- likelihood
- severity

But in RSI, those names are surface handles for deeper ordering structures.
The true dimensional object is not the label alone, but the stable direction of constraint response that the label tracks.

---

## 4. Origin of Dimensions

Dimensions do not exist prior to distinguishability and ordering.

They emerge when:

1. asymmetry creates differential viability response
2. distinguishability partitions regions
3. repeated ordering constraints appear
4. those orderings remain coherent across recurrence
5. a stable direction of variation becomes extractable

---

### 4.1 Foundational chain

\[
\text{Asymmetry} \rightarrow \text{Distinguishability} \rightarrow \text{Ordering} \rightarrow \text{Coherent direction} \rightarrow \text{Dimension}
\]

---

### 4.2 Foundational statement

> Comparison does not merely use dimensions; it helps create them.

---

## 5. Ordering as the Generator of Dimensionality

Dimensions emerge from stable ordering structure.

### 5.1 Ordering
Ordering is the placement of regions relative to one another along a direction of variation.

Examples:
- faster than
- more likely than
- less severe than
- more coherent than

### 5.2 Why ordering matters
Without ordering:
- regions may differ
- but no directional geometry exists

With ordering:
- relative position appears
- direction appears
- comparison becomes geometric
- dimensions become extractable

---

### 5.3 Foundational statement

> A dimension emerges when ordering relations become stable enough to define a persistent direction.

---

## 6. Comparison and Dimensional Emergence

Comparison is one of the clearest dimensional generators in RSI.

### 6.1 Comparison does not merely select
Comparison does not only choose a region.
It imposes directional ordering across regions.

### 6.2 Comparison creates relative geometry
A statement like:

- mobile performance is slower than desktop

does not simply describe two regions.
It imposes an ordering relation along an emergent dimension.

### 6.3 Consequence
Repeated coherent comparison patterns allow RSI to infer:
- direction
- relative position
- dimensional load
- angular alignment with other orderings

---

## 7. Dimension as Constraint Direction

A dimension can be understood as a stable direction of constraint effect across basis regions.

### 7.1 Constraint-direction view
A set of constraints contributes to a dimension when they:
- produce similar patterns of viability change
- align in their directional effects
- persist under recurrence
- preserve ordering coherence

### 7.2 Directional equivalence
Two constraints belong to the same direction when they produce proportional patterns of viability change across relevant basis regions.

This is stronger than:
- equal magnitude
- lexical similarity
- shared wording

It depends on:
- response pattern
- ordering alignment
- viability transformation profile

---

### 7.3 Foundational statement

> A dimension is a stable equivalence class of aligned viability transformations.

---

## 8. Dimensional Response Signature

Dimensions matter in RSI because basins respond across them.

### 8.1 Basin tuple relationship
In the basin tuple:

\[
B_t = (V_t,\; C_t,\; R_t,\; L_t)
\]

the dimensional component is:

\[
R_t
\]

### 8.2 Definition
\(R_t\) is the dimensional response signature of the basin.

It records:
- which dimensions are active
- how strongly the basin loads on them
- what directional profile the basin exhibits
- how the basin behaves across semantic geometry

### 8.3 Why dimensions matter for identity
A basin may change support density, wording, or spread and still remain itself if its dimensional response signature remains continuous.

---

### 8.4 Foundational statement

> Dimensional response is one of the primary carriers of basin identity.

---

## 9. Independence and Orthogonality

Dimensions are not automatically independent.

### 9.1 Independence
Two dimensions are independent when constraint responses along one do not systematically determine or distort responses along the other.

### 9.2 Orthogonality
Orthogonality is the geometric expression of dimensional independence.

Two dimensions are orthogonal when:
- their ordering structures do not systematically co-vary
- their constraint fields do not collapse into one another
- coherence along one does not require coherence along the other

### 9.3 Important distinction
Consistent ordering defines a dimension.
Non-interference between orderings defines independence between dimensions.

---

### 9.4 Foundational statement

> Independence is not merely stable ordering; it is non-interference between ordering structures.

---

## 10. Dimensional Coupling

Not all dimensions are independent.

### 10.1 Coupled dimensions
Two dimensions are coupled when:
- ordering along one predicts ordering along the other
- constraint responses systematically co-vary
- one axis partially determines another

### 10.2 Examples
Some dimensions may be:
- strongly coupled
- weakly coupled
- partially dependent by scale
- nearly orthogonal at one scale and coupled at another

### 10.3 Consequence
Dimensions form not just a flat set of axes, but a structured manifold of relations.

---

### 10.4 Foundational statement

> Dimensional hierarchy emerges from varying degrees of predictive coupling and independence.

---

## 11. Dimensional Hierarchy

Dimensions may form hierarchical structure.

### 11.1 Hierarchical intuition
Some dimensions may be:
- more primitive
- more derived
- more local
- more global
- more scale-sensitive

### 11.2 Hierarchy source
Dimensional hierarchy may emerge from:
- coupling strength
- recurrence stability
- basis dependence
- scale dependence
- explanatory reach

### 11.3 Consequence
A higher-order dimension may summarize multiple lower-order ordering structures.
A lower-order dimension may refine a broader dimensional family.

---

## 12. Dimensions and Basis Regions

Dimensions do not float above basis.
They act over basis regions.

### 12.1 Basis relationship
Basis regions are the smallest distinguishable regions under the current constraint system.

Dimensions become meaningful only when:
- basis exists
- distinguishability is active
- ordering can be tracked across basis response

### 12.2 Component view
A dimension may be represented as a stable direction of differential viability effect over basis regions.

This means that basis regions provide the effective coordinate substrate on which dimensions can be computed.

---

### 12.3 Foundational statement

> Basis regions provide the distinguishable substrate; dimensions provide the directional structure over that substrate.

---

## 13. Dimensions and Geometry

Once dimensions stabilize, semantic space becomes geometric.

### 13.1 Geometry becomes available
At that point RSI can meaningfully support:
- direction
- angle
- opposition
- orthogonality
- relative distance
- ordering chains
- dimensional load

### 13.2 Important note
Geometry is not primary.
Topology and viability come first.

Geometry emerges only when:
- ordering stabilizes
- dimensions persist
- coherence supports directional identity

---

### 13.3 Foundational statement

> Semantic geometry is emergent dimensional coherence over viability space.

---

## 14. Angle and Dimensional Alignment

Angles are derived from alignment relations between dimensions or constraint directions.

### 14.1 Intuition
If two dimensions or constraint families:
- produce proportional viability changes, they align
- produce opposing viability changes, they oppose
- do not systematically co-vary, they approach orthogonality

### 14.2 Meaning of angle
Angle expresses:
- alignment
- opposition
- independence
- partial coupling

### 14.3 Important consequence
Angle is not primitive.
It is derived from:
- directional similarity of viability transformation
- ordering coherence
- co-variation structure

---

### 14.4 Foundational statement

> Angles are derived identities of aligned or opposed dimensional response.

---

## 15. Dimensional Stability

A dimension is only real in RSI if it stabilizes.

### 15.1 Stability conditions
A dimension stabilizes when:
- ordering remains coherent across recurrence
- comparison does not collapse
- directional response persists
- basis resolution remains sufficient
- coherence remains above threshold

### 15.2 Dimensional persistence
A stable dimension must:
- recur
- remain interpretable
- preserve directional identity
- resist collapse under modest perturbation

---

### 15.3 Foundational statement

> A dimension exists only as long as it remains a stable coherent direction under recurrence.

---

## 16. Dimensional Collapse

Dimensions can fail.

### 16.1 Collapse
A dimension collapses when:
- distinctions along it are no longer resolvable
- ordering becomes redundant or indistinguishable
- constraint response becomes uniform
- coherence along the axis falls below meaningful threshold

### 16.2 Collapse causes
Dimensional collapse may occur through:
- overcompression
- symmetry saturation
- basis collapse
- inability to distinguish regions along the axis

### 16.3 Result
A previously meaningful direction becomes:
- merged
- redundant
- unresolved
- absent from the current geometry

---

## 17. Dimensional Distortion

A dimension may remain present but become distorted.

### 17.1 Distortion
Dimensional distortion occurs when:
- asymmetry exceeds current basis resolution
- ordering remains partially present
- but directional interpretation becomes unstable or unfaithful

### 17.2 Consequences
A distorted dimension may:
- fluctuate
- alias
- overreact
- lose clean angular relation to other dimensions
- produce unstable basin identity

---

### 17.3 Foundational statement

> A distorted dimension is not absent; it is present beyond lawful resolvability.

---

## 18. Primitive vs Emergent Dimensions

RSI should remain careful here.

### 18.1 Primitive dimensions
At the semantic-content level, most dimensions should be treated as emergent rather than pre-given.

### 18.2 Structural dimensions
However, RSI may later require a small set of more primitive structural dimensions governing viability behavior itself, such as:
- magnitude
- polarity
- locality
- coupling

These are not semantic-content axes like speed or severity.
They are candidate dimensions of the constraint system itself.

### 18.3 Current stance
This distinction remains provisional.
For now, RSI prioritizes emergent semantic dimensions over premature hard-coding of primitive content dimensions.

---

## 19. Dimensions and Multi-Scale Structure

Dimensions may behave differently across scale.

### 19.1 Scale sensitivity
A dimension may appear:
- independent at one scale
- coupled at another
- stable locally
- distorted globally
- collapsed at fine resolution
- preserved at coarse resolution

### 19.2 Consequence
Dimension analysis must remain compatible with:
- basis refinement
- basin overlap across scale
- scale-conditioned ordering
- multi-layer recurrence

---

## 20. Dimension Extraction (Provisional)

RSI does not yet define a final extraction algorithm, but conceptually dimensions emerge when:

1. repeated ordering constraints appear
2. those constraints remain aligned
3. the resulting directional response persists across recurrence
4. the direction remains distinguishable from other directions
5. the direction supports coherent comparison or transformation

This is enough for current theoretical scaffolding.

---

## 21. Why Dimensions Matter

Without dimensions, RSI can describe:
- viable regions
- support density
- constraint interaction

But it cannot fully formalize:
- comparison
- ordering
- angular identity
- orthogonality
- basin response regime
- semantic metric structure

Dimensions are therefore the bridge from:
- semantic topology
to
- semantic geometry

---

## 22. Provisional Summary

Dimensions in RSI are:
- emergent
- constraint-derived
- viability-relative
- ordering-based
- coherence-dependent
- basis-grounded
- potentially hierarchical
- not identical to terms

A dimension exists when a stable direction of viability transformation becomes coherent enough to function as an ordering axis.

---

## 23. Provisional One-Line Summary

> Dimensions are coherence-stabilized directions of viability transformation that emerge from persistent ordering structure across distinguishable semantic regions.
