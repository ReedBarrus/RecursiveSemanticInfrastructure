# Viability

This document formalizes viability as the central observable of Recursive Semantic Infrastructure (RSI).

Viability is the core quantity through which RSI evaluates whether semantic structure is:
- forming
- concentrating
- persisting
- differentiating
- distorting
- collapsing

Viability is not treated as truth, correctness, or empirical frequency.
At this stage, viability is structural.

---

## 1. Purpose

RSI requires a central observable that can answer:

- what semantic structure remains possible under constraint?
- what semantic structure remains coherent under recursive interference?
- what semantic structure remains stable enough to form a basin?
- what semantic structure becomes too weak, diffuse, or distorted to preserve itself?

Viability is that observable.

---

## 2. Core Definition

### 2.1 Provisional definition
Viability is the coherent density of a semantic region that survives recursive constraint interference.

This means viability is concerned with:
- survival under constraint
- concentration under interference
- coherence under recurrence
- structural persistence rather than static presence

### 2.2 What viability is not
Viability is not identical to:
- truth
- falsity
- empirical probability
- confidence
- popularity
- lexical repetition
- mere possibility without structure

Viability refers to structured survivability under semantic interference.

---

## 3. Viability as Structural Probability

RSI treats structural probability as viability-relative rather than frequency-relative.

### 3.1 Structural probability
Structural probability is the remaining viable freedom within constrained semantic possibility space.

A region becomes more structurally probable when:
- compatible constraints converge on it
- viability density concentrates in it
- competing alternatives lose support
- recurrence preserves its coherence

A region becomes less structurally probable when:
- support diffuses
- constraints conflict
- coherence weakens
- distinguishability collapses
- drift exceeds identity-preserving bounds

### 3.2 Foundational statement
> Structural probability is the viable freedom that remains after recursive constraint interaction.

---

## 4. Possibility, Constraint, and Viability

RSI distinguishes clearly between:

### 4.1 Possibility
Possibility is the available semantic space prior to or beneath current constraint closure.

### 4.2 Constraint
Constraint is the interference process that reshapes, reduces, reinforces, or orders possibility.

### 4.3 Viability
Viability is the coherent density that remains after possibility has been acted on by constraints.

This yields the basic RSI chain:

\[
\text{Possibility space} \rightarrow \text{Constraint interference} \rightarrow \text{Viability structure}
\]

---

## 5. Viability as Region Survival

A term is treated as a region in semantic possibility space.

A region is viable when it remains:
- non-empty
- coherently supported
- distinguishable
- recursively stable enough to persist under active constraints

### 5.1 Minimal viability intuition
At minimum, a region is viable if recursive constraint application does not annihilate or dissolve it into incoherent indistinguishability.

### 5.2 Stronger viability intuition
A region is strongly viable when:
- compatible support is dense
- destructive interference is limited or structured
- recurrence preserves the basin
- distinguishing structure remains resolvable

---

## 6. Viability and Asymmetry

Viability depends on asymmetry.

Without asymmetry:
- all regions respond the same
- distinguishability does not emerge
- viability remains uniform or trivial
- no meaningful basin can form

With asymmetry:
- differential viability response becomes possible
- basis regions emerge
- semantic structure becomes geometric
- coherent basins can form

### 6.1 Foundational statement
> Viability becomes informative only when asymmetry produces differential constraint response.

---

## 7. Viability and Distinguishability

Viability and distinguishability are related but not identical.

### 7.1 Distinguishability
Distinguishability asks:
- can constraints produce different responses across regions?

### 7.2 Viability
Viability asks:
- which of those regions remain coherently supported under those responses?

So:
- distinguishability produces structure
- viability selects surviving coherent structure within that differentiated field

### 7.3 Consequence
A region may be distinguishable but weakly viable.
A region may be viable only at a coarse basis and become non-viable at finer resolution.
A region may gain viability when new constraints sharpen distinguishability.

---

## 8. Viability Distribution

Viability is not a single scalar by default.
It is first a distribution.

### 8.1 Definition
A viability distribution is the structured density pattern of viability over semantic space or basis regions at a given recurrence layer.

This is represented in the basin tuple as:

\[
V_t
\]

### 8.2 Working decomposition
The current provisional decomposition is:

\[
V_t = (\mu_t,\; \Sigma_t,\; \rho_t,\; F_t)
\]

Where:

- \(\mu_t\) = viability center
- \(\Sigma_t\) = viability spread / shape
- \(\rho_t\) = viability density concentration
- \(F_t\) = fragmentation measure

### 8.3 Interpretation
#### Center \( \mu_t \)
Where viability concentrates most strongly.

#### Spread \( \Sigma_t \)
How broad, narrow, elongated, or anisotropic the viable region is.

#### Density \( \rho_t \)
How thickly coherent support is concentrated in the viable region.

#### Fragmentation \( F_t \)
How much viability is split across multiple competing subregions instead of concentrated in one basin.

---

## 9. Coherence Density

Viability is not mere surviving volume.
It is coherent density.

This means:
- a broad region with weak support may remain possible but have low viability density
- a narrow region with high support concentration may have high viability density
- negation alone may leave large remaining space but weak coherence density
- modality may preserve structure while lowering density

### 9.1 Foundational statement
> Viability is not just surviving space; it is surviving coherent density.

### 9.2 Why \( \rho_t \) matters
The viability density concentration term \( \rho_t \) is the clearest local indicator of whether structure is:
- thickening
- diffusing
- fragmenting
- collapsing

This is why RSI should maintain \( \rho \) consistently as the density notation.

---

## 10. Constraint Effects on Viability

Constraints act by transforming viability.

At the deepest level, constraints may:
- increase viable density
- decrease viable density
- redistribute viable density
- gate viability
- order viability
- anchor viability

These can be grouped into broad effect families.

### 10.1 Constructive effect
Constraint interaction reinforces viability concentration.

Examples:
- compatible projection
- confirming reference
- lawful conditioning
- aligned comparison

### 10.2 Destructive effect
Constraint interaction removes or weakens viability.

Examples:
- negation
- direct contradiction
- incompatible ordering
- unresolved opposition

### 10.3 Transformative effect
Constraint interaction redistributes viability by combining constructive and destructive effects.

Transformation preserves some continuity while reshaping the basin.

### 10.4 Foundational statement
> Transformation is redistribution of viability through simultaneous constructive and destructive interference.

---

## 11. Modality and Viability Weighting

Modality acts as directional amplitude modulation on viability.

Examples:
- may
- might
- likely
- probably
- possibly

These do not usually create new regions directly.
They alter how strongly a region is made viable or non-viable.

### 11.1 Minimal interpretation
Modality scales the magnitude of a constraint’s effect on a target region.

### 11.2 Consequences
- strong modality tightens viability concentration
- weak modality preserves broader viable freedom
- uncertainty is therefore not absence of structure, but lower-density viable structure

### 11.3 Foundational statement
> Modality is viability weighting, not structural annihilation.

---

## 12. Negation and Viability Subtraction

Negation acts primarily as destructive viability subtraction.

### 12.1 Minimal interpretation
Negation removes or opposes viability in a target region, relation, or ordering.

Examples:
- not slow
- not more likely
- not caused by

### 12.2 Important consequence
Negation alone often broadens remaining possibility while lowering coherence density.

That means:
- negation may preserve volume
- but reduce positive specification
- and therefore weaken local viability density

### 12.3 Foundational statement
> Negation removes viability; it does not by itself positively specify replacement structure.

---

## 13. Comparison and Viability Ordering

Comparison introduces ordering constraints between viable regions.

Examples:
- faster than
- more reliable than
- less likely than

### 13.1 Minimal interpretation
Comparison positions regions relative to each other along an emergent dimension.

### 13.2 Viability consequence
Comparison does not only select regions.
It helps:
- generate ordering geometry
- reveal dimensions
- reshape viability through relative positioning

### 13.3 Foundational statement
> Comparison creates or reveals viability ordering across emergent dimensions.

---

## 14. Viability Regimes

Viability can express different structural regimes.

### 14.1 High-density viability
- concentrated
- coherent
- basin-forming
- strongly supported

### 14.2 Low-density viability
- weakly concentrated
- partially open
- still structured
- often uncertain

### 14.3 Fragmented viability
- split across competing subregions
- weak local coherence
- vulnerable to chaos or branching

### 14.4 Distorted viability
- partially present
- no longer faithfully resolvable under the current basis
- asymmetry exceeds resolution capacity

### 14.5 Collapsed viability
- density falls below distinguishability threshold
- basin can no longer preserve meaningful structure

---

## 15. Viability and Failure

Viability is central to failure classification, but failure is not defined by low viability alone.

### 15.1 Noise
Diffuse low-density perturbation without stable basin concentration.

### 15.2 Uncertainty
Low-density but directionally biased structure.

### 15.3 Chaos
Low-persistence, rapidly reconfiguring viability structure.

These are field regimes.

### 15.4 Collapse
Loss of viable concentration below distinguishability threshold.

### 15.5 Distortion
Loss of faithful resolvability because asymmetry exceeds basis capacity.

### 15.6 Rupture
Discontinuous identity break across recurrence layers.

These are failure regimes.

### 15.7 Foundational distinction
> Noise, uncertainty, and chaos describe viability field conditions; collapse, distortion, and rupture describe failures of formed viable structures.

---

## 16. Viability and Coherence

Viability is necessary for coherence, but not sufficient.

A region may remain viable in a weak or temporary sense without becoming coherent.

Coherence requires that viable structure:
- remain aligned
- remain dense enough
- remain persistent across recursive application

### 16.1 Relationship
- viability asks what survives
- coherence asks what remains stably aligned under recurrence

### 16.2 Foundational statement
> Viability provides the substrate of survival; coherence provides the test of persistent agreement.

---

## 17. Viability and Persistence

Persistence is recurrence-tested viability continuity.

A viable basin becomes persistent when:
- its viability structure overlaps with itself across layers
- its generating constraints remain compatible
- its drift remains within identity-preserving bounds

### 17.1 Current recurrence rule
\[
P_t = O_t \cdot G_t \cdot (1 - D_t)
\]

Where:
- \(O_t\) = overlap across layers
- \(G_t\) = core constraint agreement retention
- \(D_t\) = normalized drift

### 17.2 Foundational statement
> Persistence is viability that continues to survive as itself under recurrence.

---

## 18. Viability and Basin Formation

A basin forms when viability becomes sufficiently concentrated and coherent to count as an emergent semantic structure.

### 18.1 Basin formation conditions
A basin tends to form when:
- viability density \(\rho\) is sufficiently concentrated
- fragmentation is limited
- constraints align
- recurrence preserves the structure

### 18.2 Basin loss conditions
A basin tends to fail when:
- density diffuses
- fragmentation rises
- destructive interference dominates
- recurrence no longer preserves identity

---

## 19. Viability and Basis Adaptation

Viability helps govern when the system should expand or compress its basis.

### 19.1 Compression tendency
When viability patterns become highly symmetric and redundantly represented, basis compression becomes possible.

### 19.2 Expansion tendency
When unresolved asymmetry causes viability distortion or loss of resolvability, basis expansion becomes necessary.

### 19.3 Foundational statement
> Basis adapts in response to viability pressure: compressing redundant symmetry and expanding unresolved asymmetry.

---

## 20. Toward a Minimal Viability Metric

Viability is first a distribution, not only a scalar.

However, RSI may still require provisional scalar summaries for experiments.

A minimal scalar viability summary might eventually depend on:

- coherent density \(\rho\)
- distinguishability
- constraint agreement
- fragmentation penalty
- persistence support

A generic conceptual form might be:

\[
V^{*} \approx f(\rho,\; \text{distinguishability},\; \text{agreement},\; 1-F,\; P)
\]

This is not yet a final formula.
It is only a guide for future experimental formalization.

---

## 21. Why Viability Matters

Without viability, RSI has:
- terms
- constraints
- asymmetry
- distinguishability

but no central observable for:
- survival
- concentration
- coherence substrate
- basin formation
- persistence testing
- failure classification

Viability is the quantity that makes RSI into a semantic dynamics framework rather than a static ontology.

---

## 22. Provisional Summary

Viability is the central observable of RSI.

It is:
- structural
- density-based
- recurrence-sensitive
- distinct from truth and empirical probability
- the substrate from which coherence, persistence, basins, and failure regimes can be formalized

Viability is best understood as:
- surviving coherent density
- under recursive constraint interference
- within a distinguishable semantic field

---

## 23. Provisional One-Line Summary

> Viability is the coherent density of semantic structure that survives recursive constraint interference and remains available for basin formation, persistence, and meaning.
