# Recursive Semantic Infrastructure

Recursive Semantic Infrastructure (RSI) is a research and engineering project for modeling language as a structured field of recursively interacting semantic regions.

At its core, RSI treats:

- **terms** as regions in semantic possibility space
- **constraints** as modes of interference between regions
- **viability** as the coherent density that survives constraint interaction
- **coherence** as persistent dynamic constraint agreement across recursive application
- **claims** as emergent basins of stable semantic structure

This project exists to formalize, test, and develop a lawful semantic substrate that can later interface with broader structural memory and dynamical systems work.

---

## Purpose

RSI is being developed to answer a foundational question:

> How can language be modeled as a geometric, recursive, and viability-driven system rather than as a flat symbolic stream?

The project aims to:

- formalize semantic structure as region topology and constraint dynamics
- define viability, coherence, persistence, and dimensionality in semantic space
- model claims as emergent coherent basins rather than fixed logical atoms
- distinguish uncertainty, noise, chaos, distortion, rupture, and collapse as lawful structural regimes
- provide a semantic framework that can later bridge into Dynamical Memory Engine (DME)

---

## Core Thesis

RSI proceeds from the following base assumptions:

### 1. Terms are regions
A term is treated as a region in semantic possibility space rather than a fixed point or token.

### 2. Language is recursive interference
Language is modeled as recursive interference among semantic regions through constraint application.

### 3. Constraints shape viability
Constraints do not merely connect terms; they reshape possibility space by increasing, decreasing, or transforming viable semantic regions.

### 4. Viability is the central observable
Semantic structure is evaluated in terms of viability: the coherent density that remains under recursive constraint interference.

### 5. Coherence is persistent agreement
Coherence is defined as the persistence of aligned constraint responses across recursive layers.

### 6. Claims are emergent basins
Claims are not primitive units. They emerge as stable or semi-stable basins of viability under recursive semantic interaction.

### 7. Dimensions are emergent directions of constraint
Semantic dimensions are not pre-given containers. They emerge as stable directions of ordering and differential constraint response.

## Working Definitions

These definitions are provisional and will be formalized in the project docs.

### Viability
The coherent density of a semantic region that survives recursive constraint interference.

### Coherence
The persistence of aligned constraint responses under recursive application.

### Persistence
The degree to which a viable basin preserves its identity, agreement, and geometry across recurrence layers.

### Basin
A coherent semantic structure formed by concentrated viability under a generating constraint field.

### Basin identity
The persistent dimensional constraint-response signature that allows a basin to remain itself through transformation.

### Drift
The differential transformation of a basin’s viability distribution, constraint composition, and dimensional response across recurrence layers.

### Overlap
The retained continuity of viable region, generating constraints, response signature, and lineage between basin states.

### Distinguishability
The ability of constraints to produce different viability responses across regions.

### Dimension
An emergent direction of constraint ordering or differential viability response.

## Minimal Coherence Model

\[
C = A \cdot P \cdot D
\]

Where:

- **A** = alignment / convergence of active constraints
- **P** = persistence across recursive layers
- **D** = compatible support density

This is a provisional model intended for conceptual and experimental testing.

## Recurrence Rule (Provisional)

A current working persistence step is:

\[
P_t = O_t \cdot G_t \cdot (1 - D_t)
\]

Where:

- **O_t** = overlap between basin states across layers
- **G_t** = retention of core constraint agreement
- **D_t** = normalized drift across layers

This rule is expected to evolve as the basin tuple is further formalized.

## Structural Regimes

RSI currently distinguishes between low-density field states and structural failure modes.

### Low-density or unstable field regimes
- **noise**
- **uncertainty**
- **chaos**

### Structural failure regimes
- **collapse**
- **distortion**
- **rupture**

This distinction is important:

- noise, uncertainty, and chaos describe the condition of the semantic field
- collapse, distortion, and rupture describe failures of formed structures under recursive persistence

## Relation to DME

RSI is currently being developed as a distinct but related project alongside Dynamical Memory Engine (DME).

The intended relationship is:

- **DME**: dynamical structural memory substrate
- **RSI**: semantic constraint and viability substrate
- **Bridge**: lawful translation layer between structural memory dynamics and semantic basin formation

RSI is not yet assumed to be a native DME subsystem. It is being developed independently so that its primitives, definitions, and experimental behavior can be stabilized before integration.
